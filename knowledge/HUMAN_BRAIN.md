

# Comprehensive Scientific Report: Anatomy, Physiology, and Electrophysiology of the Human Heart

The human heart is a highly specialized, muscular organ responsible for pumping blood throughout the circulatory system. Operating continuously over a lifetime, it beats approximately one hundred thousand times a day and circulates roughly seven thousand five hundred and seventy liters of blood daily. It is located in the thoracic cavity, nestled within the middle mediastinum between the lungs, and is roughly the size of a closed fist. The organ is protected by a multi-layered sac called the pericardium, which consists of a fibrous outer layer and a double-layered serous membrane. The narrow space between these serous layers contains pericardial fluid, which minimizes friction during contractions. Directly beneath this protective sac lies the epicardium, which is the thin, outermost visceral layer of the heart wall. Beneath the epicardium is the myocardium, the thick, muscular middle layer composed of specialized cardiac muscle cells that generate the contractile force needed to pump blood. The innermost layer is the endocardium, a smooth membrane that lines the heart chambers and covers the valves to ensure laminar, non-turbulent blood flow throughout the interior of the organ.

The interior of the heart is divided into four distinct chambers that work in coordinated pairs. The right atrium receives oxygen-depleted blood returning from the systemic circulation of the upper body via the superior vena cava and the lower body via the inferior vena cava. From the right atrium, blood passes into the right ventricle, which pumps the deoxygenated blood into the low-pressure pulmonary circulation to the lungs via the pulmonary trunk. On the opposite side of the heart, the left atrium receives oxygen-rich blood returning from the lungs via four pulmonary veins. This oxygenated blood is then transferred to the left ventricle, which pumps it into the high-pressure systemic circulation of the body via the aorta. Because the left ventricle must overcome high systemic vascular resistance to distribute blood to the entire body, its muscular wall is roughly three times thicker and more powerful than that of the right ventricle.

To ensure that blood flows in a strict, one-way direction and to prevent backflow or regurgitation, the heart utilizes four specialized cardiac valves. The atrioventricular valves prevent blood from flowing backward from the ventricles into the atria during ventricular contraction. On the right side, this role is performed by the tricuspid valve, which consists of three cusps. On the left side, the mitral or bicuspid valve performs this function using two cusps. These valves are anchored securely to the ventricular walls by tough, fibrous cords called chordae tendineae, which are attached to muscular projections called papillary muscles to prevent the valve cusps from prolapsing under high pressure. The other two valves are the semilunar valves, which prevent blood from flowing backward from the great arteries into the ventricles during ventricular relaxation. The pulmonary valve is situated between the right ventricle and the pulmonary trunk, while the aortic valve is situated between the left ventricle and the aorta.

The movement of blood through the cardiovascular system is divided into two continuous loops consisting of the pulmonary circuit and the systemic circuit. In a complete, sequential circuit, deoxygenated blood from the body enters the superior and inferior vena cava, flows into the right atrium, passes through the tricuspid valve, and enters the right ventricle. Upon contraction, the blood is pushed through the pulmonary semilunar valve into the pulmonary trunk and arteries, traveling to the lungs where carbon dioxide is released and oxygen is absorbed. The newly oxygenated blood returns to the heart via the pulmonary veins, entering the left atrium, passing through the mitral valve, and flowing into the left ventricle. Finally, the left ventricle forcefully ejects the blood through the aortic semilunar valve into the aorta, distributing oxygen and nutrients to the systemic organs of the body before the depleted blood returns to the vena cava to repeat the loop.

This rhythmic movement is driven by the coordinated alternation of muscular contraction and relaxation, known as the cardiac cycle. During the diastole or relaxation phase, the ventricles relax and expand, causing the atrioventricular valves to open and allowing blood to flow passively from the atria into the ventricles. At the end of diastole, the atria contract to push the remaining volume of blood into the ventricles. During the systole or contraction phase, the ventricles contract, causing a sudden increase in pressure that snaps the atrioventricular valves shut, which produces the first heart sound, commonly described as the lub sound. Once the pressure within the ventricles exceeds the pressure in the great arteries, the semilunar valves fly open and blood is forcefully ejected. After ejection, the ventricles relax, the semilunar valves snap shut to prevent backflow, producing the second heart sound, commonly described as the dup sound, and the cycle begins anew.

Unlike skeletal muscles, which require direct neural stimulation to contract, the heart possesses autorhythmicity, which is the innate ability to generate its own electrical impulses. This intrinsic conduction system begins with the sinoatrial node, located in the upper wall of the right atrium. Known as the natural pacemaker of the heart, the sinoatrial node spontaneously depolarizes at an average rate of sixty to one hundred times per minute, sending an electrical wave across the atria that causes them to contract. The electrical signal then reaches the atrioventricular node, positioned at the junction between the atria and the ventricles. The atrioventricular node introduces a critical delay of approximately one-tenth of a second, which ensures that the atria have completely emptied their blood into the ventricles before ventricular contraction begins. From the atrioventricular node, the signal travels down the Bundle of His, which is the sole electrical pathway connecting the upper and lower chambers. This bundle splits into the right and left bundle branches, running down the interventricular septum toward the apex of the heart. Finally, the signal spreads into the Purkinje fibers, which rapidly distribute the electrical impulse upward through the ventricular myocardium, causing the ventricles to contract in a unified wave from the bottom up to squeeze blood toward the outflow tracts.

The electrical currents generated by this conduction system can be measured on the surface of the body using an electrocardiogram. A standard electrocardiogram trace consists of three key electrical events. The P wave represents atrial depolarization, which is the spread of the electrical signal across the atria immediately prior to contraction. The QRS complex represents rapid ventricular depolarization. Because the ventricular muscle mass is so large, this wave is highly pronounced on the trace and completely masks the electrical signal of atrial repolarization, which occurs at the exact same time. The T wave represents ventricular repolarization, which is the recovery of the electrical state of the ventricles in preparation for the next heartbeat.

At the microscopic level, cardiac muscle tissue combines structural elements of both skeletal and smooth muscle to optimize continuous, high-endurance performance. The defining microscopic feature of cardiac tissue is the presence of intercalated discs, which connect individual cardiomyocytes end-to-end. These structures contain desmosomes, which act as structural anchors to bind the cells together tightly and prevent them from tearing apart during forceful contractions. They also contain gap junctions, which are microscopic channels that allow ions such as sodium, potassium, and calcium to pass freely from one cell directly to the next. This rapid ion transfer allows the electrical signal to travel almost instantly across the entire muscle mass, causing the heart to contract as a single, coordinated unit known as a functional syncytium. Additionally, cardiomyocytes are packed with mitochondria, which occupy up to thirty-five percent of the cell volume to provide the continuous supply of adenosine triphosphate required to prevent fatigue. Unlike skeletal muscle, cardiac contraction relies heavily on extracellular calcium, where an initial influx of calcium through specialized channels triggers a massive release of calcium from the internal sarcoplasmic reticulum, binding to troponin to initiate muscle contraction.

The heart does not absorb oxygen or nutrients from the blood flowing through its internal chambers, but instead relies on its own dedicated blood supply network called the coronary circulation. The coronary arteries branch directly off the base of the ascending aorta. The left coronary artery divides into the left anterior descending artery, which supplies blood to the front of the left ventricle, and the circumflex artery, which supplies the left atrium and the back of the left ventricle. The right coronary artery supplies the right atrium, the right ventricle, and the electrical conduction nodes. After oxygen is extracted by the heart tissue, deoxygenated venous blood is gathered by cardiac veins and emptied into the coronary sinus, a large vein on the back of the heart that empties directly into the right atrium.

The performance of the heart is analyzed using several fundamental physiological relationships. Stroke volume represents the volume of blood pumped out of a ventricle during a single contraction, calculated as the difference between the end-diastolic volume remaining after relaxation and the end-systolic volume remaining after contraction. Cardiac output represents the total volume of blood pumped by a ventricle in one minute and is calculated by multiplying the heart rate by the stroke volume. For a healthy resting adult, a typical heart rate of seventy beats per minute multiplied by a stroke volume of seventy milliliters per beat results in a cardiac output of approximately five liters per minute. Mean arterial pressure represents the average pressure driving blood through the systemic arteries and is roughly calculated by adding the diastolic blood pressure to one-third of the difference between the systolic and diastolic blood pressures.

When any component of this complex system is compromised, severe cardiovascular diseases can arise. A myocardial infarction, commonly known as a heart attack, occurs when a coronary artery becomes blocked by a ruptured cholesterol plaque or blood clot, depriving a region of the heart muscle of oxygen and leading to rapid cell death. Coronary artery disease is the progressive narrowing and hardening of these coronary arteries due to plaque buildup, which severely restricts blood flow over time. Arrhythmias are abnormal heart rhythms caused by malfunctions in the electrical conduction system, such as atrial fibrillation, which involves chaotic atrial activity, or ventricular fibrillation, which involves chaotic ventricular activity that stops blood flow entirely and causes sudden cardiac arrest. Finally, heart failure is a chronic condition where the heart muscle is too weak or too stiff to pump blood efficiently, causing fluid retention in the lungs or extremities.
